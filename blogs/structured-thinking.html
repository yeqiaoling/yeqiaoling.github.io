How to Talk to Executives When There’s No Clear Answer

A 5-step framework for structuring ambiguous conversations — inspired by real VP-level discussions.

By Qiaoling Ye | April 19, 2025

✨ Intro: From Executive Questions to Structured Thinking

Recently, I had the chance to speak with several senior leaders — a VP of Data Science, a CIO, and a Director of Analytics. Each conversation was unique, but they shared one key trait:

Their questions weren’t about model accuracy, dashboards, or tooling. Instead, they asked things like:

  • “How would you structure a forecasting system for seller behavior?”
  • “How would you identify healthy eaters for a new feature rollout?”
  • “How do you know your data product is actually influencing decisions?”
Can you structure ambiguity into action?
Can you create a decision path when the problem isn’t well defined?
Can you help others make better decisions — not just build better models?

🧠 What Executives Are Really Asking For

When leaders ask, “What would you do?” — they’re not expecting a perfect answer.

They’re evaluating whether you can:

  • Frame the real business goal
  • Identify the key factors involved
  • Lay out clear options
  • Understand the tradeoffs
  • Offer a principled, testable path forward

In short, they want decision collaborators — not just data producers.

🪜 A Mental Structure: The 5-Step Response Framework

After a few rounds of learning-by-doing, I now use this 5-step mental model when facing ambiguity:

  1. Clarify the goal
    “What are we actually trying to optimize or decide?”
  2. Define key variables or dimensions
    “What factors influence the outcome, and which can we observe or control?”
  3. Propose 2–3 viable paths
    “What are some reasonable approaches we could take?”
  4. Compare tradeoffs
    “What are the pros and cons of each path?”
  5. Recommend a starting point + feedback loop
    “Here’s what I’d try first — and how we’d learn from it.”

💼 Case Study: Predicting Quote Acceptance in Resale

In one discussion, a VP of DS asked how I’d approach predicting quote acceptance on a luxury resale platform — where every item is unique, sellers vary widely, and seasonality matters.

1. Goal: Predict whether a seller will accept a quote, to improve pricing accuracy, reduce delays, and optimize inventory flow.

2. Key variables:

  • Seller’s historical acceptance rate
  • Item features (brand, category, price range)
  • Seasonality and time of year
  • Time lag between quote and response

3. Possible paths:

  • Seller-level behavioral modeling
  • Item-level modeling based on brand and pricing sensitivity
  • Contextual modeling (quote timing, macroeconomic signals)

4. Tradeoffs:

  • Seller models are stable but lack generalizability
  • Item models are flexible but noisier
  • Contextual models adapt quickly but may overfit trends

5. Recommendation: I’d start with a hybrid seller-item model, layering in contextual features for calibration. We could A/B test for uplift in acceptance rate and prioritize interpretability for merchandiser trust.

🧭 Why This Matters

Building models is just part of the job. Communicating structured judgment — especially when partnering with product, marketing, or operations — is equally essential.

When done well, a decision path turns ambiguity into alignment. And I’ve noticed: when you bring clarity, the conversation shifts — from “Can you analyze this?” to “Can you help us decide?”

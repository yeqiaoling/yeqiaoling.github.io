How to Talk to Executives When There’s No Clear Answer

By Qiaoling Ye | April 17, 2024

A 5-step framework for structuring ambiguous conversations, inspired by real VP-level discussions.

Intro: From executive questions to structured thinking

Recently, I had the chance to speak with several senior leaders — a VP of Data Science, a CIO, a Director of Analytics. Each conversation taught me something different.

But there was one common thread: their questions weren’t about model accuracy, dashboards, or specific tools.

  • “How would you structure a forecasting system for seller behavior?”
  • “How would you identify healthy eaters for a new feature rollout?”
  • “How do you know your data product is actually influencing decisions?”
Can you structure ambiguity into action?
Can you create a decision path when the problem isn’t well defined?
Can you help others make better decisions — not just build better models?

What executives are really asking for

When leaders ask “what would you do,” they don’t want a fixed solution. They’re looking for your ability to:

  • Frame the real business goal
  • Identify the key factors involved
  • Lay out the options clearly
  • Show you understand the tradeoffs
  • Offer a principled starting point, not perfection

In other words, they want decision collaborators, not just data producers.

A mental structure — The 5-Step Response Framework

  1. Clarify the goal
    “What are we actually trying to optimize or decide?”
  2. Define the key variables or dimensions
    “What are the controllable and observable factors?”
  3. Propose 2–3 possible paths
    “What are some ways we could approach this, even roughly?”
  4. Compare tradeoffs
    “What would each path get us, and what would we lose?”
  5. Recommend a starting point + learning loop
    “Here’s what I’d try first, and how we’d learn from it.”

Example – Predicting quote acceptance in resale

In one conversation, a VP of DS asked me how I’d structure a prediction system for quote acceptance on a luxury resale platform — where every item is unique, sellers are diverse, and seasonality matters.

1. Goal:
Predict whether a seller will accept a quote — to reduce latency, improve inventory flow, and inform pricing strategy.

2. Key variables:

  • Historical acceptance rate (by seller)
  • Item characteristics (brand, category, price range)
  • Time of year (seasonality)
  • Time lag between quote & response

3. Possible paths:

  • Seller-level behavioral modeling
  • Item-level + brand sensitivity
  • Contextual modeling (quote timing, macro trends)

4. Tradeoffs:

  • Seller models are stable but not generalizable
  • Item models are broader but noisier
  • Contextual models are dynamic but harder to validate

5. Recommendation:
Start with a hybrid model combining seller + item factors, and layer in contextual features for calibration. Validate via A/B test on acceptance rate uplift. Prioritize interpretability for merchandising decisions.

Why this matters

Building models is only part of our job. Communicating structured judgment is just as important — especially when working across product, operations, or with executive teams.

A well-framed decision path turns ambiguity into alignment.

I’m still practicing this myself. But when I do it right, I notice the conversation shifts — from “can you analyze this” to “can you help us decide.”
